# MCENG-258 - Heat pipe modeling

Capillary-Driven Heat Pipe Modeling for Electronics Cooling
This project develops a physics-based numerical model of a capillary-driven heat pipe used to cool an electronic processor. The objective is to simulate steady-state operation of the heat pipe, predict chip temperature under different heat loads, and evaluate the physical limits of the system.
The model combines thermodynamics, fluid mechanics, and heat transfer to describe vapor flow, liquid return through a porous wick, and the capillary forces that drive the circulation of the working fluid.

Project objective
Electronic components generate significant heat that must be removed efficiently to prevent overheating. Heat pipes are widely used passive cooling devices that transport heat through evaporation and condensation of a working fluid.
This project investigates whether a capillary-driven heat pipe can effectively cool a processor by modeling:
evaporation at the heated chip interface
vapor transport through the pipe core
condensation in the cooled condenser section
liquid return through a porous wick driven by capillary pressure
The model predicts the operating conditions of the system and evaluates its thermal performance.
Modeling approach
A steady-state one-dimensional model of the heat pipe was implemented in Python.
The model solves the coupled governing relations for:
Thermodynamics
saturation pressure–temperature relation
latent heat transport
Vapor flow
vapor velocity and Reynolds number
frictional pressure losses in the vapor core
Liquid flow through the wick
Darcy’s law for porous media
viscous pressure losses
Capillary equilibrium
pressure generated by the curved liquid interface
constraint that determines the capillary limit
From these relations the model calculates:
vapor and liquid pressures
evaporator and condenser temperatures
vapor mass flow rate
capillary interface radius
processor chip temperature
Main analyses performed
Steady-state performance
The code predicts the operating state of the heat pipe for a given heat load.
For example, at 250 W heat dissipation the model determines the vapor temperatures, pressures, and resulting chip temperature.
Heat load sensitivity
The model evaluates how chip temperature and capillary interface radius change with increasing heat transfer rate.
Results show that:
chip temperature increases with heat load
the interface radius decreases to generate sufficient capillary pressure
Maximum heat transport capacity
The heat pipe has a capillary limit where the wick can no longer generate enough pressure to return liquid to the evaporator.
The model determines the maximum allowable heat transfer rate before this failure occurs.
